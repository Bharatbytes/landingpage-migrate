explore the most visited places

Hyderabad

The Charminar is an iconic landmark and a symbol of Hyderabad, built in 1591 by Sultan Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah. This majestic monument features four grand arches and a central dome, and is known for its intricate stucco decorations and bustling market area. It was originally constructed to commemorate the end of a plague and serves as a historic and cultural centerpiece.

Mumbai

Situated in Mumbai, the Gateway of India was built in 1924 to commemorate the visit of King George V and Queen Mary to India. This iconic monument features a blend of Hindu and Muslim architectural styles, with its impressive basalt arch and intricate details. It has become a symbol of Mumbai's history and a popular tourist attraction.

Goa

Located in Old Goa, the Basilica of Bom Jesus is a renowned Catholic church and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Built in the late 16th century, it is famous for its Baroque architecture and the tomb of St. Francis Xavier, whose relics are enshrined here. The church’s ornate interior and historical significance make it a key symbol of Goa’s colonial past.

Delhi

The Red Fort, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, is an imposing 17th-century fortification built by Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan. Constructed from red sandstone, it served as the main residence of the Mughal emperors for around 200 years. The fort's majestic architecture, with its massive walls and beautiful gardens, reflects Mughal grandeur and historical significance.

Meghalaya

Cherrapunji, known as one of the wettest places on Earth, is famous for its breathtaking landscapes and living root bridges. The village is renowned for its ancient practices of using the roots of rubber trees to create bridges that are both functional and environmentally sustainable. The natural beauty and unique cultural practices make Cherrapunji a significant heritage site in Meghalaya.

Rajasthan

Hawa Mahal, or the "Palace of Winds," is an iconic building in Jaipur, known for its unique façade with 953 small windows, or "jharokhas." Built in 1799 by Maharaja Sawai Pratap Singh, the structure was designed to allow royal women to observe street festivities without being seen. Its intricate latticework and pink sandstone make it a striking example of Rajput architecture.
